The AICP Bid Form Explained: A Working Producer's Complete Guide

Everything you need to know to build, submit, and protect a commercial production bid — written for producers who are already in the field.

Date March 24, 2026 Read 12 min read By Chris Taylor

The AICP bid form is the standardized budget template used by commercial production companies to estimate and present production costs to advertising agencies. Created by the Association of Independent Commercial Producers, it organizes every expense on a commercial shoot — from pre-production labor through post-production finishing — into labeled sections (A through X) that agencies expect to see on every bid they review.

If you're a working line producer or production coordinator bidding on commercial jobs in the United States, the AICP form is the language you speak. Not knowing how to build one properly doesn't just cost you the job — it costs your company money when line items get missed, fringes get miscalculated, and contingency gets negotiated away before you've even started prep.

This guide is written for producers who are already in the field. It's not an introduction to filmmaking or a glossary of terms. It's a practical walkthrough of how the AICP bid form works, where budgets actually break, and how to build a bid that protects your production and wins the job.

What Is the AICP Bid Form and Why Does It Exist?

The AICP bid form is a standardized budget document that breaks commercial production costs into clearly defined categories so that advertising agencies, production companies, and clients can compare bids on equal terms. The Association of Independent Commercial Producers introduced it in the 1970s to bring consistency to a process that was, at the time, a mess of incompatible spreadsheets and handwritten estimates.

Before the AICP form existed, every production company submitted bids in their own format. Agencies couldn't do apples-to-apples comparisons. A DP might be buried in "crew costs" on one bid and itemized separately on another. The AICP form solved this by assigning every production expense to a specific section — always in the same order, always with the same labels.

Today, the AICP bid form is the default format for commercial production bidding in the United States. When an agency puts a job out for triple-bid (the standard practice of soliciting three competing bids), they expect each production company to return an AICP-formatted estimate. Some agencies will accept modified formats for digital-first or social content, but for broadcast national spots, regional campaigns, and any union shoot, AICP is non-negotiable.

The form is maintained and updated by the AICP, with the most recent bidding reference guide published in February 2026. The official blank bid form is available as an Excel spreadsheet from AICP.com.

How Are the 24 Sections (A–X) Organized?

The AICP bid form contains 24 sections, labeled A through X. Each section covers a distinct cost category. Here's how they break down at a high level:

Pre-Production and Shooting Labor (Sections A–B)

Section A: Pre-production and Wrap Labor. This covers all crew labor before the camera rolls and after the shoot wraps — the line producer's prep days, production coordinator time, location scouting labor, and wrap days for closing out the production. It also includes the production designer's prep and art department pre-production labor.

Section B: Shooting Labor. Every crew member working on shoot days goes here. DP, gaffer, key grip, sound mixer, script supervisor, hair and makeup, wardrobe, art department — all listed by day rate, number of days, and overtime estimates.

Production Expenses (Sections C–K)

Section C: Props, wardrobe, and animals purchased or rented for the production.

Section D: Studio and location costs — rental fees, permits, location fees, site restoration, parking, and security.

Section E: Travel and living expenses — flights, hotels, per diems, ground transportation, and shipping for crew traveling to location.

Section F: Equipment rental — camera packages, lighting, grip, generators, specialty rigs, and expendables.

Section G: Film stock, hard drives, and digital media (historically a larger line item when productions shot on film).

Section H: Miscellaneous production costs — messengers, phone, copying, office supplies, and other overhead that doesn't fit elsewhere.

Section I: Production insurance — general liability, equipment, errors and omissions, workers' comp, and vehicle insurance.

Section J: Director and creative fees — the director's creative fee, prep days, travel days, and any attached creative team. This is an above-the-line cost.

Section K: Talent costs and casting — SAG-AFTRA session fees, fitting fees, agency commissions, casting director fees, and extras/background. Residuals and use fees are typically handled separately in the talent contract, not on the bid form.

Post-Production (Sections L–W)

Sections L through W cover the editorial and finishing pipeline: offline editing, online/conform, color correction, visual effects, music (original composition or licensing), sound design, audio mix, and deliverables. Each phase gets its own section so agencies can see exactly where post dollars are going.

Markup and Contingency (Section X)

Section X is where the production company's fee lives. It includes the production fee (markup) — typically calculated as a percentage of below-the-line costs — and the contingency, which is a reserve for unforeseen expenses. This section is where your profit margin is defined, and it's often the first thing agencies try to negotiate down.

How Do Fringe Costs Compound Your Labor Spend?

Fringes are the silent budget killer on every commercial production. They're the mandatory employer-paid contributions — payroll taxes, union pension and health, workers' compensation, and vacation pay — that get added on top of every crew and talent day rate. If you're not calculating fringes correctly, your labor sections are wrong from the start.

Here's how they break down for a typical union commercial production:

Payroll Taxes and Workers' Comp (All Crew)

Every employee on your payroll — union or not — incurs payroll fringes. FICA (Social Security + Medicare) is 7.65% of gross wages, FUTA and SUTA add another 1–3% depending on state, and workers' compensation insurance for production crew typically runs 4–8% of gross wages. Combined, payroll fringes alone add roughly 13–18% to every labor dollar.

SAG-AFTRA Pension and Health

For SAG-AFTRA talent on a commercial, the employer contributes 20.5% of gross wages to the SAG-AFTRA pension and health fund. This is on top of the session fee. It's not optional, it's not negotiable, and it applies to every principal performer, voiceover artist, and on-camera talent covered under a SAG commercial contract.

IATSE Health, Pension, and Vacation

For IATSE crew on a commercial production agreement (CPA), the employer contributes to health, pension, and vacation funds. These rates vary by local — in Los Angeles (Local 600, Local 80, Local 728), combined fringe contributions typically run 14–18% of gross wages. The 2025 CPA agreement included a 4% base wage increase in year one, which also raised the dollar amount of fringe contributions.

The practical impact: a DP at $3,500/day doesn't cost you $3,500. After IATSE fringes (~16%) and payroll taxes (~14%), that DP costs you approximately $4,550/day — a 30% increase over the rate you negotiated. On a 2-day shoot with a 12-person union crew, fringe costs can add $15,000–$25,000 to your labor sections.

How Do Markup and Contingency Actually Work?

Section X of the AICP form is where the production company's compensation lives, and it's the section most misunderstood by people outside production. It contains two components: the production fee (markup) and the contingency.

The Production Fee (Markup)

The standard production fee is 25% of below-the-line costs. This is the production company's gross margin — it covers office overhead, staff salaries, insurance, and profit. A common misconception is that 25% markup means 25% profit. It doesn't. After overhead, most commercial production companies net between 3% and 15% on a given job.

Important: the production fee is typically calculated on the BTL sub-total only. Above-the-line costs (director fees, talent) are passed through to the client without markup. Some agencies allow markup on talent; most don't. This distinction matters because it directly affects your bottom line.

"A common misconception is that 25% markup means 25% profit. It doesn't. After overhead, most commercial production companies net between 3% and 15% on a given job."

Contingency

Contingency is a reserve for unforeseen production costs — weather delays, equipment failure, overtime, last-minute location changes, or any of the hundred things that go sideways on a shoot. The industry standard is 10% of the BTL sub-total.

Here's the critical thing about contingency: if you don't use it, some agencies expect it back. Others treat it as part of the production company's compensation. This should be clarified in the bid letter before the job starts. Experienced producers negotiate to keep unused contingency because it compensates for the risk they're managing.

When agencies push back on markup, they're often looking at that 25% number without understanding what it covers. A good bid letter explains exactly what's included in the production fee: office overhead, production insurance, accounting and payroll processing, executive producer supervision, and the company's profit. Transparency on this point wins more bids than cutting your rate.

How Do You Fill Out Each Section Without Missing Line Items?

The most practical advice I can give on filling out the AICP form: work backwards from the shoot. Start with Section B (shooting labor) because that's your biggest crew commitment. Figure out every person who needs to be on set each day, then work outward — what do those people need in prep (Section A)? What equipment do they require (Section F)? Where are they working (Section D)?

Section A: Pre-Production and Wrap Labor

Start with the line producer (that's you). How many prep days do you realistically need? For a 2-day national spot, plan on 8–12 prep days and 3–5 wrap days. Then build out the production coordinator (usually 2–3 more days than the LP on both ends), production designer prep, and any department heads who need pre-production time.

The mistake that haunts every budget: underestimating wrap days. You need time to return equipment, close out locations, reconcile receipts, and file paperwork. Budget at least 3 wrap days for the LP and coordinator. One day is never enough.

Section B: Shooting Labor

List every crew member by role, day rate, and number of shoot days. Include overtime estimates — on a 12-hour shoot day, you're going to hit meal penalties and overtime for most crew. A conservative estimate is 2 hours of OT per shoot day for key crew. For IATSE crew, overtime rates are 1.5x for the first 2 hours past 8 and 2x after that.

Sections C–K: The Production Expense Trap

Sections C through K are where budgets go wrong because they're full of line items that are easy to forget. A non-exhaustive list of things producers commonly miss:

  • Location restoration (Section D) — the cost to return a location to its original condition. If art department paints a wall, someone has to paint it back.
  • Craft services and catering (Section H or D) — feeding a 25-person crew for 2 shoot days plus prep. Budget $75–$100 per person per day for catering, plus craft services at $500–$800/day.
  • Parking and basecamp (Section D) — in cities like LA or New York, production parking can run $2,000–$5,000 per day.
  • Equipment expendables (Section F) — tape, gels, diffusion, batteries, and the hundred small items grip and electric burn through every day. Budget $1,500–$2,500 per shoot day.
  • Hard drives and data management (Section G) — shooting 4K or 6K on a 2-day shoot generates terabytes of data. Budget for drives, a DIT, and backup media.

Post-Production: Don't Shortchange the Finish

Sections L through W cover editorial through delivery. A common mistake is budgeting for the offline edit but not properly accounting for color correction, sound design, and audio mix as separate line items. Each of these is typically a separate vendor with separate day rates. For a national spot, budget $3,000–$5,000 for color, $2,500–$4,000 for sound design, and $2,000–$3,500 for the final audio mix.

What Are the Mistakes That Blow Up Commercial Budgets?

After years of building AICP bids, the mistakes that actually cost you money are predictable. They're not exotic — they're the same oversights on every job.

1. Underestimating Pre-Production Days

New producers budget 3–5 prep days for the LP on a national spot. Experienced producers budget 8–12. The difference is knowing how long it actually takes to scout locations, negotiate vendor deals, coordinate with the agency, cast talent, and build a shooting schedule. Pre-production is where you save money on the shoot — cutting it short means surprises on set.

2. Forgetting That Fringes Compound

Payroll taxes on top of union pension on top of health contributions on top of workers' comp. Each one is calculated on gross wages, and together they add 25–40% to your base labor cost. If you're building a budget with raw day rates and adding fringes as an afterthought, you're already underwater.

3. Treating Contingency as Padding

Contingency is not padding — it's risk management. Padding is adding a few hundred dollars to each line item. Contingency is a defined reserve for things you can't predict. When an agency asks you to remove contingency, they're asking you to absorb 100% of the risk for things neither of you can control. Push back on this.

"Contingency is not padding — it's risk management. When an agency asks you to remove contingency, they're asking you to absorb 100% of the risk for things neither of you can control."

4. Not Accounting for Versions and Deliverables

A 30-second national spot rarely ships as a single deliverable. You'll need a :30, a :15 cutdown, possibly a :06 bumper, social edits (16:9, 9:16, 1:1), and sometimes multiple language versions. Each version requires editorial time, color, sound, and QC. Budget for deliverables or you'll be eating those costs out of your margin.

5. Lowballing to Win the Bid

The cheapest bid doesn't always win — but it always loses money. Agencies triple-bid to ensure fair pricing, not to find the cheapest option. A bid that's 20% below the competition signals that you don't understand the scope, not that you're offering a deal. Price your bid honestly, explain your numbers, and let the work speak.

6. Missing the Bid Letter Details

The AICP bid form doesn't stand alone — it ships with a bid letter that spells out assumptions, exclusions, and terms. If your bid assumes 2 shoot days but the agency is thinking 3, that's a conversation the bid letter should start. Always itemize what's included and what's excluded (residuals, re-edits beyond X rounds, weather day coverage, travel for client video village).

Frequently Asked Questions About the AICP Bid Form

What is the AICP bid form?

The AICP bid form is the standardized budget template created by the Association of Independent Commercial Producers for estimating and presenting commercial production costs. It organizes every production expense into labeled sections (A through X) covering pre-production labor, shooting crew, equipment, locations, talent, post-production, markup, and contingency. Advertising agencies in the United States require it as the default format when soliciting production bids for television, digital, and branded content commercials.

How many sections does the AICP bid form have?

The AICP bid form contains 24 sections labeled A through X. Each section covers a specific cost category: Section A is pre-production and wrap labor, Section B is shooting labor, Sections C through K cover production expenses like locations, equipment, and materials, Section J is the director and creative fees, Section K is talent and casting, Sections L through W cover post-production (editorial, music, finishing), and Section X covers the production fee (markup) and contingency.

What are AICP fringes?

AICP fringes are the mandatory employer-paid contributions added on top of crew and talent day rates. They include payroll taxes (FICA, FUTA, SUTA), workers' compensation insurance, and union benefit contributions such as SAG-AFTRA pension and health (currently 20.5% of gross wages) and IATSE health, pension, and vacation funds. Fringes typically add 20% to 40% to base labor costs depending on union status and jurisdiction, and they must be calculated and shown separately on the AICP form.

How do I calculate contingency on an AICP budget?

Contingency on an AICP budget is typically calculated as a percentage of the total below-the-line production costs (everything except director fees, talent, and the production fee itself). The industry standard is 10% of the sub-total of Sections A through K. Contingency appears in Section X alongside the production fee. It exists to cover unforeseen costs like weather days, overtime, or equipment failures. Some agencies negotiate contingency down to 5% or cap it at a fixed dollar amount — but experienced producers know that cutting contingency is cutting your safety net.

What is the difference between above-the-line and below-the-line on an AICP form?

On an AICP bid form, above-the-line (ATL) costs are the creative and directing fees — primarily the director's fee in Section J and talent costs in Section K. Below-the-line (BTL) costs are everything else: crew labor (Sections A and B), production expenses (Sections C through I), and post-production (Sections L through W). The distinction matters because the production fee (markup) is typically calculated as a percentage of below-the-line costs only, and contingency is also applied to the BTL sub-total.

What is a wrap cost and where does it appear on the AICP form?

Wrap costs are the labor expenses incurred after the shoot ends but before post-production begins — returning equipment, striking sets, closing out locations, filing paperwork, and reconciling receipts. Wrap labor appears in Section A (pre-production and wrap labor) of the AICP form. A common mistake is budgeting generously for pre-production but underestimating wrap days. Most productions need 2 to 5 wrap days for the line producer and production coordinator, and 1 to 2 days for the production designer and art department to return materials.
