    Commercial DP Day Rate 2026: What Line Producers Actually Budget

    A mid-level DP in the LA non-union market runs $1,200–$1,600 per day before fringe, kit fee, and camera package. Here's how to budget the full number correctly.

    Date April 1, 2026 Read 7 min By Villager Pro

    A mid-level DP in the LA non-union market runs $1,200–$1,600 per day before fringe, before kit fee, and before you even look at a camera package. That number climbs past $5,000 the moment you're in agency-facing territory or calling a name. If you're building a budget and guessing, you're either leaving money on the table or blowing the bid before the call sheet exists.

    The DP line in your budget is deceptively simple. It reads like a single number. It isn't. What a DP charges, what they bring, and what you still have to rent are three different things that live in different sections of your budget and are taxed differently. Getting that distinction right is the difference between a clean bid and a conversation you don't want to have with your EP on day two.

    What a Commercial DP Day Rate Actually Includes

    The DP day rate is a labor number. It covers the cinematographer's time, creative direction of the image, and anything they personally own and bring to set under a kit fee arrangement. It does not cover the camera body, lenses, or any rental equipment, unless you've made an explicit deal otherwise.

    Labor vs. gear: what's in the rate and what's a separate line

    The day rate pays for the DP's presence, skill, and creative contribution. The camera package is a rental that typically shows up in your equipment section, quoted either through a rental house or through the DP's own package deal. These are separate lines. They're fringed differently. A DP who quotes you an "all-in" rate covering their labor and their package is consolidating two budget lines for convenience. Understand what's inside that number before you accept it.

    Kit fee vs. camera package: how to budget both without doubling up

    A kit fee, typically $100–$300 per day, covers the DP's personal tools: light meters, lens cloths, small accessories, monitors they personally own. It is not a camera package. It is not fringed. If a DP also owns their camera and you're renting it from them, that camera rental is a separate line item, quoted at day-rate market value regardless of who owns the gear.

    Prep days and wrap days: when they're expected and what they cost

    Most commercial DPs on any spot over $75,000 expect at least one prep day, sometimes two, for tech scouts, equipment checks, and location prep. Prep days typically bill at the full day rate. Do not budget half-days for prep on a working DP. If they're on the clock doing real work, they're billing full rate.

    Union DP Rates: IATSE Local 600

    IATSE Local 600 represents camera department crew on union productions. On a signatory commercial, you're working with negotiated rates, not a simple published scale. The scale floor for commercials is approximately $1,400–$1,600 per day for a 10-hour day, but most established union DPs on agency-facing work negotiate above scale.

    Negotiated rates for union commercials in the LA market typically run $2,000–$4,000 per day, depending on the DP's track record and the production's budget tier. The union also adds pension, health, and vacation contributions on top of the gross rate, which changes your fringe calculation entirely. If you're budgeting a union shoot, get the current Local 600 commercial agreement and build from the actual scale, not from memory.

    How Fringe Applies to DP Labor

    In California on a non-union production, budget fringe at 24% of gross labor. This covers employer-side payroll taxes, workers' compensation, and related costs. Fringe applies to the day rate. It does not apply to the kit fee, any equipment rentals, or vendor costs.

    This distinction matters more than it sounds. A DP who charges $1,500/day plus a $200 kit fee is billing two separate things. Fring the $1,500. Do not fringe the $200. If you're rolling the kit fee into the gross labor number and applying fringe to the combined total, you're over-budgeting your fringe line and potentially creating a discrepancy when the production report comes in.

    Budgeting a DP Across Different Spot Sizes

    Spot size determines not just the DP rate but the entire support structure around them. A $50,000 spot with a single-day shoot has a very different DP equation than a $300,000 agency job with three shoot days and a pre-production meeting at the agency's offices.

    $50K spot: what's realistic

    At this budget, you're working with an emerging-to-mid-level DP, likely someone building their commercial reel. Figure $750–$1,100/day, one to two shoot days, possibly no separate prep day. Camera package will be modest: $400–$800/day from a rental house or the DP's own gear. Total DP labor for a two-day job: $2,400–$3,400 before fringe. Fringed: $2,976–$4,216.

    $150K spot: mid-tier DP with full camera package

    This is where you're calling mid-level to senior DPs. Budget $1,400–$2,000/day for labor. A three-day shoot with one prep day = four billable days = $5,600–$8,000 in gross DP labor before fringe. Fringe adds $1,344–$1,920. Camera package is now a meaningful line: $900–$1,800/day from a rental house, handled separately. The Farallon team builds budgets in this range regularly for Bay Area and national clients, and the DP labor section alone can run $8,000–$12,000 fully loaded once you account for prep and fringe.

    $300K+ spot: when you're calling agents

    At this level, you're likely talking to agents, not the DP directly. Rates for established DPs with agency relationships start at $2,500/day and have no real ceiling for name cinematographers. Budget conservatively at $3,000–$4,500/day, build in prep days, and get a deal memo before the budget is locked.

    Common Budgeting Mistakes with DP Labor

    These are the errors that show up in budgets consistently, regardless of experience level.

    Forgetting prep days. A DP on a shoot with any meaningful complexity needs prep time. If you don't budget it, they'll either ask for it (and you'll scramble to find the money) or they'll show up without prep (and you'll regret it). Budget one prep day minimum on any job with multiple locations, specialty equipment, or agency oversight.

    Frinching the kit fee. The kit fee is not labor. It doesn't get fringed. Period. If your payroll provider or accountant wants to know why the fringe line looks the way it does, the answer is that you correctly separated labor from equipment. An audit that finds fringe on a kit fee is a problem you created in the budget.

    Not distinguishing camera op from DP on smaller jobs. On a sub-$75,000 production, sometimes you're hiring a camera operator who also DPs. Sometimes you're hiring a true DP who also operates. These are different market positions and they price differently. A camera operator running a DP day rate is a budget line you should look at. A DP operating their own camera is standard. Know which one you're booking.

    The Bottom Line

    The commercial DP line in your budget is three separate numbers: labor, kit fee, and camera package. Fringe applies to labor only. Kit fee and equipment rentals sit clean in their own sections. A mid-level LA DP on a three-day $150K spot costs you roughly $5,580–$9,600 in loaded labor, not counting the camera package. Build it right the first time and the bid holds up when the job goes to cost report.
