    Commercial Gaffer Day Rates 2026: LA, NY, and How to Budget Key Lighting Labor

    A single department. Before anyone turns on a light. Here's where that $12,958 comes from, and how to build the full G&E labor picture in your budget.

    Date April 3, 2026 Read 7 min By Villager Pro

    Most producers budget a gaffer and think they've covered their lighting. They haven't. The gaffer is a labor line. The lighting package is equipment. The generator is a rental. These are three separate budget sections, fringed differently, tracked differently, and collapsing them into one number is how a $150,000 commercial turns into a $160,000 problem at the wrap.

    On a $150,000 spot with a five-day shoot and two prep days, the fully loaded G&E labor alone — gaffer plus best boy electric, before anyone touches a fixture — runs $12,958. That's one department, two people, and that number doesn't include a single light, a generator, or a foot of cable. Understanding the full picture starts with understanding the difference between the labor section and the equipment section of your G&E budget.

    What the Gaffer Does (and What You're Buying)

    The gaffer is the head of the electrical department on a commercial production. They execute the DP's lighting vision, manage the electrical crew, and are responsible for the safety and function of everything that has power running through it. They are not the same as the lighting package. They are not responsible for sourcing the lights unless you've made a specific deal for that.

    The gaffer vs. the DP: who controls what

    The DP decides what the light looks like. The gaffer figures out how to make that happen with real equipment in a real space. On a well-run commercial, these two are in close communication from the tech scout forward. The DP calls for a 10K through a diffusion frame at 45 degrees. The gaffer knows whether that's in the truck, whether they need to order it, and what it costs to run at that location. That expertise is what you're paying for.

    Why the gaffer rate is a labor number, not a lighting number

    The gaffer's day rate does not include the lighting package. On the vast majority of non-union commercials, the gaffer is a hired laborer, not a vendor providing equipment. The lighting package comes from a rental house, quoted separately, and lives in the equipment section of your AICP budget. Some senior gaffers own trucks and will quote a combined labor-plus-equipment rate. If you get that offer, make sure you understand what's in the package, what isn't, and whether the combined number is competitive with separating the two.

    What's included in a typical gaffer day vs. what's always extra

    A gaffer day rate covers their time and expertise. It does not cover the best boy electric (a separate labor line), the generator (a separate rental), or the lighting fixtures (a separate rental). If you've budgeted one number for "lighting" and called it a day, you've missed at least three lines.

    Union Gaffer Rates: IATSE Local 728

    On a signatory production, your G&E labor falls under IATSE Local 728. The union scale floor for a gaffer on a commercial is approximately $650–$700 per day on a 10-hour minimum. In practice, most union commercials with any real budget negotiate above scale. Negotiated rates in the LA market run $1,000–$1,800/day for a working-to-senior gaffer.

    Union shoots also carry pension, health, and vacation fringe on top of the gross rate, which is significantly higher than the 24% non-union standard. Get the current Local 728 commercial agreement and run your fringe math against actual scale before you lock your budget. Assuming 24% fringe on a union shoot will undercut your labor section.

    Best Boy Electric: The Other Budget Line You Forget

    The best boy electric is the gaffer's second-in-command. They manage the cable runs, assist with rigging, keep the truck organized, and handle logistics so the gaffer can focus on the floor. On any commercial with more than a single-person electrical department, you need a best boy. Budget accordingly.

    In the LA non-union market, a best boy electric bills $450–$650/day on a working basis. A more experienced best boy or one stepping up to gaffer-adjacent responsibilities might push $650–$750/day. Best boy electric is a separate labor line from the gaffer, fringed separately, and should show as its own line in your G&E crew section, not lumped into the gaffer number.

    "Gaffers are labor. The truck is equipment. These are different lines, they're taxed differently, and confusing them is how you under-bid a $150K job."

    — Commercial line producer, LA market

    What Doesn't Get Fringed: Gear, Trucks, and Generator

    This is where budget errors compound. The lighting package, the generator, and the G&E truck are equipment rental lines. They live in the equipment section of your AICP budget, not the labor section. They are not fringed. If you're tracking fringe on your gaffer day rate and accidentally folding in the lighting package, you're miscalculating your fringe line and misrepresenting the cost structure of the production.

    Lighting package benchmarks for a $150K spot: a small-to-medium LED and HMI package runs $800–$1,800/day from a rental house. A full lighting package for a more ambitious spot runs $2,500–$4,500/day. Generator rental adds $400–$800/day depending on size and whether you're pulling it from a rental house or using a vendor who supplies and operates it. These numbers go in the equipment section. Clean and separate.

    Regional Rate Notes

    Atlanta and Chicago gaffers bill meaningfully lower than LA, which matters when a national brand job ends up shooting in a secondary market. An Atlanta gaffer at $500–$750/day versus a senior LA gaffer at $1,100–$1,400/day is a real budget difference on a multi-day shoot. If you're bringing an LA gaffer to Atlanta, budget LA rates plus travel and per diem. If you're hiring locally, verify the rate cards for that market rather than scaling down your LA numbers by feel.

    The calculus on bringing an LA gaffer to a secondary market usually comes down to the DP's relationship and the complexity of the lighting plan. An LA gaffer who has worked with your DP on 20 jobs is often worth the travel cost. A local Atlanta gaffer with strong commercial credits is a legitimate option that saves real money on a medium-size job.

    What a Good Gaffer Costs You, All In

    The gaffer's day rate is the starting point, not the full picture. On a $150K spot in the LA non-union market, a fully loaded G&E labor line runs close to $13,000 before you touch a single piece of equipment. Add the lighting package at $1,500/day, a generator at $600/day, and an electrician or two on shoot days, and the total G&E section can run $25,000–$35,000 on a spot of this size.

    Build the labor and equipment sections separately. Fringe the labor. Don't fringe the gear. When someone asks why your fringe line is what it is, you'll have a clear answer.
