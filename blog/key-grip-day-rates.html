    Key Grip Day Rates for Commercial Production 2026: What to Budget for Grip Labor

    One department. No truck. Before you touch a single piece of equipment. Here's what $6,262 buys you and how to build the rest of the grip section correctly.

    Date April 7, 2026 Read 7 min By Villager Pro

    The key grip is the person who makes the DP's vision physically possible. Dollies, sliders, jibs, rigging, C-stands, apple boxes, the bounce card that's keeping the shadow off the product. They don't touch a light. They don't own the truck on most jobs. And their rate, while similar to a gaffer's on paper, comes from a completely different budget section with different equipment dependencies.

    Key grips and gaffers bill within $50–$100 of each other on most non-union commercials, but the work they do and the gear that follows them is completely different. A key grip in LA runs $650–$900 per day on a working basis, up to $1,200–$1,400 for a senior with a grip truck. On a 3-day spot with one prep day, loading those numbers through 24% fringe gets you to $6,262 for the labor section alone. The dolly, the truck, and any specialty rigging are separate lines you haven't budgeted yet.

    What a Key Grip Does on a Commercial Shoot

    The key grip heads the grip department. They're responsible for everything that physically supports or modifies the camera and controls light through non-electrical means. Dollies, sliders, cranes, jibs, camera cars, rigging structures, and all the flags, nets, diffusion frames, and silks that shape light without generating it. If the DP needs to put a camera somewhere complicated or control a shadow without a dimmer, it's a grip problem.

    Camera support: dollies, sliders, jibs, and rigging

    The key grip operates or supervises the dolly on most commercial sets. They also set up sliders, jibs, cranes, and any rigging that puts a camera in a non-standard position. This work requires spatial problem-solving, equipment knowledge, and real physical coordination with the DP and camera department. A key grip who has worked with your DP before is not a luxury on a technical job. It's logistics.

    Lighting support: flags, nets, diffusion, and frames

    Everything that shapes light without a switch runs through the grip department. C-stands, flags, diffusion frames, bounce boards, silks. The gaffer designs the lighting scheme; the grip department supports it physically. On a complex lighting setup, grip and electric are working in the same space on overlapping problems. The key grip and gaffer need to be in close communication from tech scout forward.

    Why the key grip and gaffer are always in the same room

    Gaffer and key grip are the two most interdependent department heads on a commercial shoot. The lighting package is useless without the stands, frames, and flags to control it. The grip truck is useless without light to shape. Budget them together, but keep them as separate lines. They fringe the same way; they bill the same market rate; they are not the same job.

    Union Key Grip Rates: IATSE Local 80

    On a signatory production, the grip department falls under IATSE Local 80. Union scale for a key grip on a commercial runs approximately $650–$700 per day on a 10-hour minimum. In practice, established union key grips on agency-facing work negotiate above scale, and the real range for negotiated rates in LA runs $1,000–$1,800/day.

    Union productions also carry pension, health, and vacation fringe contributions above the gross rate, significantly exceeding the 24% non-union standard. If you're budgeting a union shoot and applying 24% fringe to the grip department, you're underestimating. Pull the current Local 80 commercial agreement and build from actual scale.

    Best Boy Grip: The Budget Line That Often Gets Cut First

    The best boy grip is the key grip's second-in-command. They manage the grip truck inventory, coordinate with the rental house, prep the equipment, and handle logistics so the key grip can focus on the floor. On a commercial with any meaningful grip work, a best boy is not optional. If you cut this line to save money, you've added those hours to the key grip's day and reduced your department's capacity.

    In LA, a best boy grip bills $450–$650/day non-union. An experienced best boy stepping into more responsibility might push $650–$700/day. Like the gaffer's best boy electric, this is a separate labor line, fringed separately, and should appear as its own entry in your grip crew section. Not a single "grip department" total.

    "The key grip doesn't own your lighting. But if the key grip's truck isn't there, your DP can't light the shot. Budget both. Separately."

    — Commercial line producer, LA market

    Grip Equipment: What's Labor and What's a Rental

    The key grip's day rate is a labor number. The grip truck, the dolly, the crane, and the expendables are rental lines that live in the equipment section of your AICP budget. They are not fringed. If you're calculating fringe on a number that includes grip equipment costs, you're wrong.

    In Villager Pro's AICP layout, grip labor lives in Section C and grip equipment sits in a separate rental section. When fringe runs on labor, it doesn't touch the truck or the dolly. That separation matters when someone asks why your fringe line looks the way it does.

    Equipment benchmarks for grip on a commercial shoot:

    • Grip truck rental (mid-size, LA market): $500–$900/day
    • Dolly and track rental: $400–$800/day
    • Technocrane or jib: $1,200–$2,500/day (separate line)
    • Expendables (flags, tape, sandbags): $200–$600 per shoot

    These numbers go in the equipment section. None of them get fringed.

    Key Grip vs. Gaffer: Rate Comparison and Budget Relationship

    Grip and electric are the two largest crew departments on a commercial, and they bill at comparable rates. In the LA non-union market, a working gaffer and a working key grip are often within $25–$50/day of each other. At the senior level, the ranges overlap almost entirely: $1,000–$1,400/day for both.

    Role Working Rate (LA Non-Union) Senior Rate (LA Non-Union)
    Gaffer $600–$850/day $1,000–$1,400/day
    Key Grip $600–$850/day $1,000–$1,400/day
    Best Boy Electric $450–$650/day —
    Best Boy Grip $450–$650/day —

    The rates are symmetric. The equipment costs are not. A lighting package for a $150K spot runs $1,500–$4,500/day from a rental house. The grip truck for the same shoot runs $500–$900/day. The gaffer's equipment section costs significantly more. When you add up the full G&E section, the asymmetry comes from the lighting budget, not the labor.

    Budget grip and electric labor symmetrically. Budget grip and electric equipment based on what the DP's plan actually requires. Then keep both sections clean, separate, and correctly fringed.

    What a Key Grip Really Costs You, All In

    A three-day commercial in LA with a working key grip and best boy costs $6,262 in loaded labor. Add a grip truck at $700/day for three days: $2,100. Add a dolly and track at $600/day for two shoot days: $1,200. Add $400 in expendables. Your full grip section for a three-day spot is approximately $9,962 before any specialty rigging, cranes, or camera support beyond the basics.

    That's a real number. It belongs in your budget before the bid goes out, not as a surprise on cost report. Build the labor section correctly, apply fringe only to gross labor, and let the equipment section carry the rental costs clean.
