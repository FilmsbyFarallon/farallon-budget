    Production Coordinator Rates for Commercial Production: What to Budget in 2026

    The rate isn't what makes the PC budget wrong. It's how many days you book them, when you bring them on, and whether you've budgeted the prep work that keeps a shoot from falling apart.

    Date April 5, 2026 Read 7 min By Villager Pro

    The production coordinator is the last person producers think about when building a budget and the first person who notices when the budget is wrong. In the LA non-union market, a working PC bills $350–$500 per day on a freelance basis. But the rate is almost never what makes the difference. It's how many days you book them, when you bring them on, and whether you've actually budgeted for the prep work that keeps a shoot from falling apart.

    A coordinator who starts two days before the shoot is not the same thing as a coordinator who starts ten days out. The budget difference between those two scenarios is $3,500–$5,000 in loaded labor. On a $150,000 commercial, that's real. Underestimating PC days is one of the most consistent ways production office labor ends up over-budget on cost report, not because the rate was wrong, but because the day count was optimistic from the start.

    What a Production Coordinator Does on a Commercial

    The production coordinator manages the logistics infrastructure that makes a commercial shoot possible. Contracts, travel, permits, call sheets, vendor coordination, location agreements, crew paperwork, and the thousand small problems that show up between the award of the job and wrap day. They are not an assistant director. They are not a UPM. They are the person who knows where everything is and whether it's confirmed.

    Pre-production: the invisible work before day one

    Most of what a PC does is invisible on shoot day because they did it already. Crew deals are out and countersigned. The location has a permit. The rental house confirmed the package. The hotel rooms are booked and match the call sheet. None of this happens by accident. It happens because someone with a production coordinator title worked through a checklist for two weeks and resolved every open item. Budget for that time, because it's real.

    Production: on-set vs. office roles

    On shoot day, some PCs stay in the office. Others come to set in a support capacity. On a multi-day commercial with a full crew, you often want both, which means you need an office PA or a second coordinator, not just the one PC. Know what role you're hiring for before you write the rate card. An on-set coordinator and an office coordinator are different animals, even if their day rates are similar.

    Wrap: why this is where coordinators get shorted

    Wrap is not free. Someone has to collect all the signed releases, reconcile the petty cash, return the rentals, close out the vendor invoices, and prepare the cost report. On a $150K spot, that's two to three days of real work. Budget them. If your PC is done the moment the cameras go into cases, you'll find out on cost report that nobody wrapped the production.

    Freelance Day Rate vs. Salaried PC: How to Think About Each

    For most commercial productions, the PC is a freelancer hired per project. You pay a day rate, they show up for the run of the job, and when the job wraps they move on to the next one. That's the standard model for independent production companies and most mid-size agencies.

    Freelance PC: when it makes sense and how to rate card it

    Freelance works when your project volume is inconsistent. You're not bidding three jobs a week all year; you win a job, staff up, execute, and repeat. The day rate covers everything, fringe applies to gross labor, and there's no benefits overhead outside of California employer taxes and workers' comp. Budget the day count carefully, because a day rate that looks reasonable multiplied by 15 days looks very different from one multiplied by 8.

    Weekly rate vs. daily rate: how the math changes

    Many experienced PCs will negotiate a weekly rate rather than quoting daily. In LA, a working-to-experienced PC running $400–$500/day might offer a weekly rate of $1,800–$2,200 for a week of full-time prep. That's a slight discount to daily billing that acknowledges the block commitment. For the production company, the weekly rate is cleaner to track and often slightly cheaper than seven individual day billings. Either way, confirm in the deal memo which rate structure applies and what a "day" means: is prep a half-day or a full day?

    How Many Days Does a PC Actually Work?

    This is where PC budgets go wrong. The day rate gets budgeted correctly, but the day count gets optimistic, and the coordinator bills 14 days on a job that was budgeted for 8. Every extra day at $500/day is $620 loaded. Four unexpected days is $2,480 you didn't have.

    On a $150,000 commercial with a two-to-three-day shoot, a realistic PC day count looks like this: 6–8 prep days (including a couple of partial days and one heavy push week), 2–3 shoot days, and 2–3 wrap days. That's 10–14 billable days on a job that might feel like a small spot. If you budgeted 6 days, you're already short.

    Fringe on PC Labor

    California non-union fringe runs at 24% of gross labor. A PC billing $500/day for 12 days generates $6,000 in gross labor and $1,440 in fringe, for a loaded cost of $7,440. That's the number that matters in your actual budget, not the $500 day rate.

    Phone reimbursements, mileage reimbursements, and kit stipends, if any, are not labor. They don't get fringed. A PC who asks for $50/month phone reimbursement on a project basis is submitting a non-labor expense, not adjusting their day rate. Track it separately.     PC vs. Production Manager: Where One Ends and the Other Begins

    On a small-to-mid commercial, a production coordinator can handle most of the logistics function without a UPM above them. The coordinator manages the production office, handles vendor relationships, and coordinates with the AD and DP on scheduling logistics. On a larger or more complex job, especially one with multiple locations, specialty permits, or a large traveling crew, you need a UPM or production manager above the PC, not instead of them.

    The production manager or UPM handles crew negotiations, budget oversight, schedule approvals, and the relationship with the agency or brand. The PC handles execution. These are not the same job. If you're using a PC in a PM role to save money, you're either asking too much of the coordinator or under-delivering on the production management function. Know the difference before you write the org chart.

    Rate comparison: a working production manager in LA bills $650–$900/day non-union. An experienced UPM runs $900–$1,400/day. If your job needs a PM and you budgeted for a PC, the gap is $150–$900/day in gross labor, multiplied by every day that person is on the clock.

    Salaried Production Coordinator: What the Market Pays

    If your production company is doing consistent enough volume to justify a full-time coordinator, the salaried model makes sense. In LA, base salary for a staff PC runs $55,000–$80,000 annually, depending on experience. In New York, $65,000–$90,000. In Atlanta and Chicago, $42,000–$70,000.

    But base salary isn't the real number. In California, fully loaded employer costs add 20–35% above base for payroll taxes, workers' compensation, and health benefits. A $70,000 base salary costs the production company $84,000–$94,500 per year in actual outlay. If you're doing the math on whether to staff up or keep using freelancers, model it against your actual job volume, not just the base salary comparison.

    What a Good PC Actually Costs You

    On a $150,000 non-union commercial in LA, an experienced PC working 12 days at $500/day costs $7,440 loaded. That's a real number that belongs in your production office labor section. A PC who's been in the industry for eight years and runs a clean production office earns every dollar of that rate by preventing problems you'll never hear about.

    Budget the day count honestly, apply 24% fringe to gross labor, and track non-labor reimbursements separately. Get the deal memo signed before prep week starts.
